Cloudseeker Mission

The Dream

In 2027, Project Nephelis will hurl the Cloudseeker miniprobe to Venus, hitching a ride on a SpaceX rocket. Starting today, 10:10 PM PDT, Jul 03 2025, it will dance at 55 km, navigating the tempest of Venus’s clouds for 30 days, snagging imagery and data on CO2, sulfuric acid, winds, and elusive biosignatures like phosphine. A 3U CubeSat orbiter relays it back, fueling our Venusian dreams—all for under $500,000, driven by volunteers and your donations, empowered by StellarSpark, with trajectory insights from Grok. Dive deeper at NASA’s Venus portal or The Planetary Society.

The Why

“Life forms on the surface, forced to adapt, might have migrated into the skies,” mused Carl Sagan in 1967, dreaming of Venus’s clouds. Phosphine detections tease microbial life, making Venus a cosmic enigma. ESA’s EnVision reveals its Earth-like past, undone by a greenhouse catastrophe. Cloudseeker defies billion-dollar gatekeepers, proving a scrappy team can pierce the veil.

The Tech

Envision a 1-meter balloon, forged from acid-proof polytetrafluoroethylene, gliding through Venus’s golden haze, inspired by Venera’s descent resilience. Its 1-kg sensor pod—packing a 4K camera, temperature/pressure sensors, and an autofluorescing nephelometer—unlocks atmospheric secrets, aiming to map like Magellan’s radar. Thin-film solar cells power it, riding 200 km/h winds, while a 4-kg CubeSat orbiter with a high-gain antenna beams data home. Built on Soviet Vega’s legacy but honed for 2026, Cloudseeker is lean, fierce innovation.

Our Path to Venus

Cloudseeker’s voyage is a defiant leap, powered by tech and a global tribe. Track our journey from Earth to Venus’s clouds and join the rebellion.

Technical Feasibility

Cloudseeker’s 10-kg payload—a 1-kg balloon probe and 4-kg CubeSat orbiter—targets Venus’s 55-km cloud layer, where pressure (0.5 bar) and temperature (~30°C) mirror Earth. Its polytetrafluoroethylene shield defies sulfuric acid, with solar cells and a radio sustaining 30 days. The CubeSat, a proven 3U design, relays data via NASA’s Deep Space Network. SpaceX’s rideshare, at ~$5,000/kg, keeps launch costs under $50,000, with volunteers driving down build expenses.

Launch Windows (2025–2030)

Venus windows open every ~19 months: summer 2026, January–March 2027, August–October 2028. Cloudseeker aims for January 2027, riding a SpaceX Transporter to LEO, then a lunar flyby and 5–6-month cruise, arriving June 2027.

Route to Venus

From LEO, Cloudseeker’s Photon bus climbs, slingshots past the Moon, and arcs toward Venus, tracing a path through the cosmic haze. The probe detaches 30 minutes before entry, floating at 55 km for 30 days of data collection.

Vision for Venus

“We’re not just probing Venus’s clouds—we’re building a home in the sky,” says Project Nephelis’ founder. Cloudseeker lays the foundation for floating habitats at 50–55 km, where Earth-like conditions beckon, inspired by Venera’s rugged surface glimpses. Drawing from NASA’s HAVOC, these solar-powered platforms could shelter thousands, shielded from acid by cutting-edge materials. Cloudseeker’s data—winds, composition, stability—paves the way for robotic probes and self-assembling stations, inching us toward permanent skies.
